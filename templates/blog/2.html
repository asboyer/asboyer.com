Philosophy

I was introduced to philosophy by my 10th grade English teacher, Mr. Vonnegut. In his class, we would read literate and then explore philosophy to determine if the particular book agreed or disagreed with certain schools of thought. For example, I remember our class discussing if Of Mice and Men supported or questioned Marxism.
Throughout the year, we were posed with this question: "what is the good life?" Our first assignment was called "The Good Life Vignette" where we were tasked to write a brief description illustrating what we would want our life to look like in twenty years [1]. Each essay, book, and activity always circled back the central question of the good life and how to achieve it.

I learned that literature gives us implicit answers to big questions [2]. We can derive our own meaning from 1984. "Maybe George Orwell was trying to tell us..." On the other hand, philosophy gives us explicit answers to these questions. Philosophers directly tell us their beliefs and what they believe is the optimal way to live and think. They don't subtly hide or layer their ideas into novel or play [3].
