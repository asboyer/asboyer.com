Philosophy

I was introduced to philosophy by my 10th grade English teacher, Mr. Vonnegut. In his class, we would read literature and then explore philosophy to further discuss what we read. As we studied novels such as Of Mice and Men and Brave New World, we learned philosophies ranging from Existentialism and Friedrich Nietzsche to Plato and the tripartite soul.
Throughout the year, I was posed with this question: "what is the good life?" The first assignment was called "The Good Life Vignette", where we were tasked to write a brief description illustrating what we would want our life to look like in twenty years [1]. Each essay, book, and activity always circled back to the central question of the "good life" and how to achieve it.
Over the course of the year, I learned that literature gives us implicit answers to big questions [2]. We can derive our own meaning from 1984. "Maybe George Orwell was trying to tell us..." On the other hand, philosophy gives us explicit answers to these questions. Philosophers directly tell us their beliefs and their answers to big questions. They don't subtly hide or layer their ideas into novel or play [3].
Philosophy literally means "love of wisdom." We look to philosophy (we philosophize) to understand fundamental truths about ourselves and the world. Personally, I turn to philosophy when I'm feeling lost and need not only some kind of straightforward direction, but a why. Why should I do this instead of this? Why is this meaningful? Why is this painful?
The further I explore philosophy, the more I understand "the good life". The good life is not a destination, it is not something we achieve by making more money, gaining more friends, or receiving more recognition. The good life is in how we live now.
While many great philosophers have their own how, one particular school of thought sticks out to me most: Stoicism.
